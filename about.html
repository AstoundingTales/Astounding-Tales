FROM THE EDITOR'S DESK

They used to reject us because we were too strange.

I sat across from men in decent suits who could smell a circulation number from three floors away, and I listened to their polite regrets. Too loud. Too quiet. Not quite right for us. We were humans arguing with humans about the messy business of what it meant to be alive.

I miss those days. I really do. Because somewhere along the line, the rejection stopped being about strangeness and started being about friction.

The writers I brought them — the ones who kept me awake, who made me miss my stop on the train — had too much friction. Their voices were jagged. They made mistakes. They sounded like people. And the industry realized they didn't need people anymore. They just needed the shape of a person. They sanded down the edges. They found algorithms that understood what had already sold, which is to say, they built machines that were very good at perfectly synthesizing the past. The magazines got thicker, glossier, and empty. A flawless, continuous stream of noise.

I always wondered what they thought they were reading.

The readers I knew wanted to be shaken. They wanted fiction that arrived like a phone call at three in the morning — the kind where you know before you answer that something has changed.

Science fiction's true purpose was never to predict the flying cars; it was to make the present legible. To hold the accelerating strangeness of being alive right now up to the light and say: look. Pulp science fiction did this once. Magnificently. On cheap paper that yellowed before the decade was out, this disreputable little genre told more truth about the human condition than anything published between respectable covers. It was written fast, paid poorly, and largely forgotten. But the ink smeared on your thumbs. You knew a hand had put it there.

Astounding Tales exists for the same reason speakeasies existed. Because the thing you actually want has been made unavailable through official channels.

The writers and artists in these pages have nowhere else to go because they refuse to be smoothed out. They are the friction. They are paying attention, which is rarer than it sounds.

Inside you will find a serialized novel, short fiction that bleeds when you cut it, art that refuses to sit quietly on the page, and somewhere in all of it — if you're listening — the three a.m. phone call. I'm tired. But I am still listening. Something has changed. Pick up…

Reginald Hollis
Editor, Astounding Tales
